Plain-English overview

2x Talent Assessment

A faster, fairer way to hire. Software does the heavy lifting at the start — sorting through hundreds of applicants — so our team spends its time only on the people most likely to be a great fit. It's built on tools the company already has.

Status: Idea / planning Audience: Everyone (no jargon) Updated: 06-21-26
The idea What's broken today How it would work The tools Step by step What's decided Time & effort

01The idea, in one minute

When we post a job, we can get around a thousand applicants. Today a person reads every single resume by hand — that's slow, and good candidates wait a long time (or never hear back). The idea is to let software handle the first, biggest sort automatically and fairly, then have our people spend their time only on the final handful. Same hiring software we use now, just doing more of the work for us.

02What's broken today

How we hire now

  • We post a job and applications pile up in our hiring software.
  • Someone reads every resume by hand to find the good ones.
  • Promising people get a phone call to check they come across well.
  • The best of those get interviews, and we pick someone.

Why it's painful

  • Reading hundreds of resumes by hand is the big bottleneck.
  • The whole thing takes a long time.
  • Lots of applicants never hear back — bad experience, bad for our brand.
  • The early sorting is all manual, even though software could do it.

03How it would work

Think of it as a funnel. We start with everyone who applies and narrow down to one hire — with the computer doing the wide, repetitive part and people doing the judgment part at the end.

1000 → 200
A quick test
Everyone takes a short online aptitude test. People who score too low are automatically (and politely) declined by email — no one reads a thousand resumes by hand anymore.
Automatic
200 → 20
Try the actual work
The people who pass do a small, real task from the job. The computer checks how well — and how quickly — they did it.
Automatic
20 → 5
Values & role fit
A person reviews the top candidates against our values and what the job really needs — using the same checklist for everyone, so it's consistent and fair.
Mostly automatic
5 → 1
Interview & hire
The final few meet the team in person. We choose the one — the way we always have, just with far less time wasted getting here.
People decide

04The tools — in plain terms

Nothing new to buy. These are all things the company already has; we'd just connect them so they work together.

The hiring software (Greenhouse)
Where job applications come in and candidates are tracked. Today it's basically a filing cabinet — we'd let it do more of the work automatically.
The online test (Criteria)
A short aptitude test we already pay for. We'd connect it so it's sent automatically and the scores are recorded for us.
The automation tool (n8n)
A tool we already use that quietly passes information between our apps — like sending the right email at the right moment, or moving a candidate to the next step.
The AI assistant (Claude)
An AI we already use. Here it writes friendly candidate emails and grades the "try the work" task against a clear, consistent guide.

About the personality questionnaire

We also have a personality-style questionnaire (Insights). It's genuinely useful for helping new hires settle in and for team fit — but it is not used to decide who gets hired, because it isn't designed for that. We'd only use it after someone is hired.

06What we've decided — and what we're still figuring out

The plan so far (a starting point)

  • Keep our current hiring software — just add automation around it.
  • Use tools we already have — nothing new to buy.
  • Automatically decline the lowest-scoring applicants so people aren't reading every resume.
  • Have candidates try the real work early — it's the best signal of who's good.
  • Use the personality questionnaire only after hiring, never to screen people out.
  • Email candidates at every step — no more silence.

Still to sort out

  • Turning on the link between our hiring software and the test — this is the main thing that sets the timeline.
  • A privacy check, since an AI would be handling applicant information.
  • Setting the test's pass mark so it's fair and doesn't screen out good people.
  • Whether to keep the phone call step or only use it occasionally.

A fairness note

Automatic decisions need a human keeping an eye on them, especially at first — so we don't accidentally turn away great people who don't fit the usual mold.

07How long, and how much work

The surprising part: building it is the easy bit. What actually sets the timeline is waiting on other people — switching on the connection to the test, and getting a privacy sign-off. Almost none of the wait is "doing the work."

If things move quickly
~1 week

The test connection is easy to switch on, the right people respond fast, and no heavy privacy review is needed.

More likely
~2–3 weeks

Switching on the test connection takes some back-and-forth, or a privacy review is required first.

The very first improvement — candidates getting automatic email updates — can be live almost right away, since it doesn't depend on anyone else. That alone fixes the loudest complaint.
