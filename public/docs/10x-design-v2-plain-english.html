Our hiring system vs. the Lightspeed plan

The same comparison as the technical write-up, but with the jargon stripped out. In short: we already built a big, full-featured hiring system (we call it "PA"). For Lightspeed, we designed something much smaller and simpler. This explains where they overlap, where the Lightspeed plan does something extra, and all the things our big system does that the Lightspeed plan leaves out.

01The short version

Think of it like comparing a fully-equipped commercial kitchen to a smart home-kitchen setup. Both can cook a great meal. One can also run a restaurant.

Our system ("PA") — the commercial kitchen

  • A complete, in-house hiring system we built from scratch
  • Handles the whole journey — from posting a job to making the offer
  • Used by several companies at once, with lots of controls
  • Powerful, but big and expensive to build and maintain

The Lightspeed plan — the smart home kitchen

  • A lightweight setup for one company (Lightspeed)
  • Focuses on the busy first step: sorting lots of applicants down to a few
  • Built by connecting tools Lightspeed already pays for
  • Fast and cheap to set up — about a week or two of work

The takeaway in one sentence

The Lightspeed plan gets the main job done — sort the flood of applicants, keep candidates informed, and save people's time for the final few — using only a tiny slice of what our big system can do, because it borrows tools that are already in place instead of building everything from the ground up.

02Why this is really a comparison of two different sizes

This is the most important thing to understand before the details. Our system and the Lightspeed plan aren't really rivals doing the same job — they're built for very different scopes.

  • Our system manages the entire hiring process for multiple companies: writing and approving job openings, testing candidates, running several rounds of interviews, checking references, working out pay, and getting offers signed off.
  • The Lightspeed plan only automates the front end — taking a large pile of applications and quickly narrowing it to a handful of strong people, then handing those few to a person for a final interview.

So the comparison is naturally lopsided

There's only a short list of things the Lightspeed plan does that our system doesn't — and a long list of things our system does that the Lightspeed plan simply doesn't try to do. That imbalance is the main finding, not a flaw in either one.

03How the Lightspeed plan works, step by step

Imagine 1,000 people apply for a job. Here's how the plan narrows that down to one hire, doing the early, repetitive work automatically and saving people for the end.

Everyone applies
Step 1
When someone applies, they automatically get a friendly "thanks, here's what happens next" email.
1,000 → 200
A quick online test
Applicants take a short skills-and-aptitude test. Those who score too low are automatically and politely declined.
200 → 20
A real work task
The remaining people do a small, true-to-the-job task. An AI assistant grades it for speed, accuracy, and quality.
20 → 5
A values check
A short questionnaire checks how well each person fits the company's values and the role. A person reviews the results.
5 → 1
The interview
The final few meet a real person from HR for an interview. The AI helps prepare good questions.

What's doing the work behind the scenes

The plan keeps Lightspeed's existing hiring software as the home base, adds a quiet "automation helper" that moves candidates from one step to the next, uses an AI assistant to grade tasks and write the emails, and plugs in the testing tool Lightspeed already pays for. Nothing new has to be bought — it's mostly a matter of wiring the existing pieces together.

The one thing that sets the timeline

The build itself is quick. The real wait is turning on the connection to the testing tool and getting a privacy/security sign-off for handling applicant information.

05The bottom line

Our system and the Lightspeed plan are built for very different jobs, using opposite philosophies. Ours is a big, custom platform that runs hiring end-to-end for several companies. The Lightspeed plan is a small, fast setup that speeds up the crowded early stage by connecting tools that are already there — and saves people for the final interview.

What our big system could borrow

  • The early, AI-graded "do the real task" step
  • The "connect what we already have" mindset — less building

Where the Lightspeed plan could grow later

  • Reference checks, pay, and formal offers
  • More structured scoring and multi-company support
  • These show the natural next steps if Lightspeed ever needs more than fast early screening

In plain terms

The Lightspeed plan reaches the main goal — sort the crowd, keep candidates in the loop, and save people's time for the finalists — with a small fraction of what our big system can do, simply because it reuses existing tools instead of building a whole platform. Whether that's "enough" depends on what Lightspeed actually needs — it isn't a knock on either one.

06The few names, in plain words

If you bump into these terms elsewhere, here's what they mean.

PA (our system)
The complete, in-house hiring software we built ourselves.
The Lightspeed plan
The lightweight hiring-automation design we created for the company Lightspeed.
Greenhouse
The off-the-shelf hiring software Lightspeed already uses to track applicants.
The aptitude test (Criteria / CCAT)
A short online test of thinking skills, used to screen out the lowest scorers automatically.
The automation helper (n8n)
A behind-the-scenes tool that automatically moves a candidate from one step to the next.
AI assistant (Claude)
The AI that grades tasks, writes candidate emails, and helps interviewers prepare.
Values questionnaire (Insights Discovery)
A short survey that gauges how well someone fits the company's values and the role.
Work-sample task
A small, realistic job task that shows whether a person can actually do the work.
Back-channel reference
A reference the candidate didn't list — someone contacted discreetly for a fuller picture.
